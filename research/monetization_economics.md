## 1. Sports Betting Affiliate CPA Rates

CPA (cost per acquisition) is what a sportsbook pays you per referred customer who signs up and makes a qualifying deposit/bet.

| Sportsbook | Typical CPA Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| DraftKings | $100-$300 | Higher in competitive/new markets, lower in mature ones |
| FanDuel | $100-$250 | Similar tier to DraftKings |
| BetMGM | $75-$200 | Often slightly lower than DK/FD |
| Caesars/William Hill | $75-$200 | Aggressive during launch periods |
| PointsBet | $50-$150 | Smaller book, lower CPAs |
| BetRivers | $50-$125 | Mid-tier |
| Fanatics Sportsbook | $100-$250 | Newer entrant, spending aggressively |

**Key context:**
- CPAs spike in newly-legal states (launch period) - can hit $300-$500+ temporarily
- CPAs have been compressing as the market matures (peaked 2021-2022)
- "Qualified" usually means first-time depositor (FTD) with a minimum deposit ($10-$25)
- Some programs require a minimum first bet ($5-$50) instead of just a deposit
- Top-tier affiliates with high volume negotiate custom rates above published ranges
- Small creators typically start at the lower end of these ranges

## 2. Revenue Share Models

Instead of (or in addition to) CPA, some programs offer a percentage of the sportsbook's net revenue from your referred players.

| Model | Typical Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue share (% of net gaming revenue) | 20-35% | "Net revenue" = player losses minus bonuses, adjustments |
| Hybrid (CPA + rev share) | $50 CPA + 15-25% rev share | Common for mid-tier affiliates |
| Lifetime rev share | 20-30% | Rare, most cap at 1-2 years |
| Sub-affiliate commissions | 5-10% | Earn on affiliates you recruit |

**Key context:**
- Rev share sounds great but is volatile. A few lucky players can make your revenue negative in a given month
- Most programs have "negative carryover" - if your referred players win big one month, that negative balance carries forward
- CPA is generally better for smaller affiliates because it's predictable
- Rev share favors large affiliates who send enough volume that variance smooths out
- Some programs claw back rev share if referred players are unprofitable over time
- The "net" in net gaming revenue is key - sportsbooks deduct bonuses, free bets, taxes, and sometimes platform fees before calculating your share

## 3. Better Collective Acquires Action Network

**Price: ~$240 million USD** (DKK 1.6 billion)

- **Announced:** April 7, 2022
- **Closed:** May 26, 2022
- **Buyer:** Better Collective A/S (Danish company, publicly traded on Nasdaq Stockholm)
- **What they got:**
  - Action Network brand and website (actionnetwork.com)
  - ~8 million monthly unique visitors (at the time)
  - Proprietary odds comparison and bet tracking tools
  - Established sportsbook affiliate relationships
  - Editorial team producing betting content
  - Mobile app with real-time odds and line movement
  - Player Props tool and other analytics features
  - Email newsletter with large subscriber base
- **Revenue context:** Action Network was generating ~$30-40M annual revenue at time of sale, primarily from affiliate commissions
- **Multiple:** Roughly 6-8x revenue
- **Why it matters:** This was the largest pure-play sports betting media acquisition in the US, validating the affiliate model at scale. Better Collective already owned other gambling affiliate properties globally.

## 4. Gambling Affiliate Networks (Accessible to Smaller Creators)

### Networks that work with smaller creators:

| Network/Program | Min Requirements | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Betting Partners (DraftKings) | No strict minimum | DraftKings' official affiliate program, relatively accessible |
| FanDuel Affiliate Program | Low barrier | Direct sign-up through Impact.com |
| BetMGM Affiliates | Moderate | Managed through Paysafe/Income Access |
| Revenue Pilots | Varies by brand | Multi-brand network, some brands accept small affiliates |
| Affiliate Guard Dog members | Varies | Directory of vetted programs |
| Income Access | Moderate | Paysafe-owned network, many sportsbook brands |
| Bet365 Affiliates | Low-moderate | Global brand, accepts content sites |

### Realistic path for a small creator:
1. **Start with direct programs** - DraftKings and FanDuel both accept small affiliates through their Impact.com-based programs
2. **Build a track record** - Even 5-10 conversions/month gets you noticed
3. **Negotiate up** - Once you have 3-6 months of data showing conversions, request higher CPAs
4. **Add more books** - Layer on BetMGM, Caesars, etc. as your audience grows
5. **Consider a network** - Networks like Revenue Pilots or Income Access give you access to multiple brands through one dashboard

### Minimum traffic realities:
- Most programs don't have a hard minimum, but below ~5,000 monthly visitors you'll struggle to generate meaningful conversions
- Social media followers (Twitter/X) count as "traffic" for application purposes
- A niche audience with high intent converts much better than raw numbers
- Sports betting content has high commercial intent - even a small, engaged audience can convert

## 5. Twitter/X Sports Account Sponsorship Rates

Based on available data (Meltwater research and industry benchmarks):

| Follower Count | Estimated Rate Per Sponsored Post | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1,000 | ~$2-10 | Essentially no market here |
| 5,000 | $10-50 | Micro-influencer territory |
| 10,000 | $50-150 | Starting to be viable for niche sponsors |
| 25,000 | $150-400 | Sweet spot for niche sports brands |
| 50,000 | $400-1,000 | Attractive to sportsbooks, DFS platforms |
| 100,000 | $1,000-2,500 | Solid rates, multiple sponsorship tiers possible |
| 250,000 | $2,500-5,000 | Premium niche territory |
| 500,000 | $5,000-10,000 | Major brand interest |
| 1,000,000 | $10,000-25,000+ | Top-tier sports accounts |

**Key context:**
- Twitter/X pays MUCH less per follower than Instagram, YouTube, or TikTok
- Engagement rate matters more than follower count - a 10K account with 5% engagement is worth more than a 50K account with 0.5%
- Sports betting advertisers pay a premium over general advertisers because of high customer LTV
- Thread-based content (analytics, breakdowns) drives higher engagement than single tweets
- Most small accounts won't get direct sponsorship inquiries - you need to pitch brands or join influencer marketplaces
- Niche credibility (actual analytics, not just hot takes) commands a premium in sports

### More realistic for a small account:
- **Affiliate links** in tweets and bio will likely outperform sponsored post income for accounts under 25K followers
- **Bundle deals** (tweet + newsletter mention + thread) are easier to sell than standalone tweets
- **Recurring sponsors** (monthly retainers) are more sustainable than one-off posts

## 6. Newsletter Sponsorship CPMs

CPM = cost per 1,000 subscribers (or sometimes per 1,000 opens)

| Newsletter Size | Typical CPM (per send) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| General content | $10-30 CPM | Broad audience, lower intent |
| Niche/B2B | $30-75 CPM | Higher for specialized audiences |
| Finance/investing | $50-100 CPM | High commercial intent |
| Sports betting/DFS | $40-80 CPM | Sportsbooks pay premium CPMs |
| Premium niche (small, engaged) | $50-100+ CPM | Quality > quantity |

### What that looks like in practice:

| Subscriber Count | Open Rate | CPM | Revenue Per Send |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1,000 | 40% | $50 | $20-50 |
| 5,000 | 35% | $50 | $88-175 |
| 10,000 | 30% | $50 | $150-500 |
| 25,000 | 28% | $50 | $350-1,250 |
| 50,000 | 25% | $40 | $500-2,000 |

**Key context:**
- CPM is typically calculated on OPENS, not total subscribers. A 10K list with 40% open rate = 4,000 opens
- Sports betting advertisers are among the highest-paying newsletter sponsors
- Frequency matters: 2x/week newsletter = 2x the sponsorship inventory
- Mix of ad formats: primary sponsor ($$$), secondary sponsor ($$), classified ads ($)
- Platforms like beehiiv, Sparkloop, and Paved connect newsletters with advertisers
- Below 1,000 subscribers, newsletter sponsorship income is negligible
- The real money at small scale is in AFFILIATE links within your newsletter, not display sponsors

## 8. Data Licensing Economics

### What the big companies charge:
- **Sportradar:** Enterprise contracts typically $50K-$500K+/year. They serve major media companies, sportsbooks, and leagues. Not accessible to individuals.
- **Stats Perform (Opta):** Similar enterprise tier. $100K+/year for real-time data feeds.
- **Baseball Savant/Statcast:** Free for personal/editorial use. Commercial licensing through MLB requires negotiation.
- **FanGraphs:** Open data for editorial, but commercial use requires permission. They don't really license data.

### Can an individual sell data products?
**Yes, but the opportunity is in derived insights, not raw data.** Raw data is largely commoditized or freely available (baseball especially). The value-add is:

| Product Type | Realistic Price Point | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Spreadsheet/model downloads | $5-50 one-time | Projection models, draft tools |
| Data dashboards (SaaS) | $5-20/month | Interactive tools, custom queries |
| API access to proprietary models | $25-100/month | Projections, ratings, win probability |
| Custom analysis/consulting | $50-200/hour | Team-specific analysis, draft prep |
| Seasonal data products | $20-100/season | Draft guides, auction calculators |

### Realistic individual data product examples:
- Fantasy baseball projection systems (Steamer, ZiPS started as individual projects)
- Ottoneu-specific value calculators (your wheelhouse)
- Betting model outputs (win probabilities, player props)
- Spring training / prospect data compilations
- Historical database access with custom interfaces

## 9. SaaS Metrics for Sports Subscription Products

### Churn rate benchmarks:

| Product Type | Monthly Churn | Annual Churn | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| General SaaS (B2C) | 5-7% | 45-55% | Consumer products churn higher |
| Media/content subscriptions | 6-10% | 50-70% | Sports content is seasonal, churn spikes in offseason |
| Sports data/tools | 7-12% | 55-75% | Heavy seasonality - baseball = 6-month active season |
| Fantasy sports tools | 10-15% | 70-85% | Extremely seasonal, many cancel post-draft |
| Premium newsletters | 5-8% | 45-60% | Lower churn if community/engagement is strong |

### LTV calculations for a hypothetical sports subscription:

**Assumptions:** $10/month price, 8% monthly churn

- Average customer lifetime: 1/0.08 = 12.5 months
- LTV = $10 x 12.5 = $125
- If CAC (customer acquisition cost) is $20-40, that's a healthy 3-6x LTV:CAC ratio

**Seasonal adjustment:** Many sports subscriptions should use annual billing to reduce churn:
- $10/month x 12 = $120/year vs. $80-100/year annual price
- Annual subscribers retain at 2-3x the rate of monthly subscribers
- Offering a "season pass" (March-October for baseball) at $50-70 is a common model

### Revenue modeling for a solo sports product:

| Scenario | Subscribers | Price | Monthly Revenue | Annual Revenue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Side project | 100 | $10/mo | $1,000 | $12,000 |
| Growing | 500 | $10/mo | $5,000 | $60,000 |
| Full-time viable | 1,000 | $10/mo | $10,000 | $120,000 |
| Success | 2,500 | $12/mo | $30,000 | $360,000 |

**Reality check:** Getting to 500 paying subscribers for a niche sports product is very achievable. Getting to 2,500 requires real marketing effort and a genuinely useful product.

### Common patterns in successful solo sports businesses:

1. **Start with free content** that demonstrates expertise (Twitter threads, blog posts, free tools)
2. **Build email list** early - this is your most valuable asset
3. **Productize your analysis** - turn recurring work into a subscription or tool
4. **Layer revenue streams:**
   - Free content drives traffic
   - Affiliate links (sportsbooks, DFS) on free content = baseline revenue
   - Newsletter sponsorships as audience grows
   - Premium subscription product for your best analysis/tools
   - Consulting or custom work for high-value clients
5. **Seasonality is your enemy** - find ways to generate off-season revenue or go multi-sport
6. **Niche down aggressively** - "baseball analytics" is better than "sports," but "pitcher analysis" or "Ottoneu-specific tools" is even better to start

## Key Takeaways for a Solo Baseball Analytics Creator

1. **Affiliate income is the fastest path to revenue.** Even a small, engaged audience on Twitter/X can generate meaningful sportsbook affiliate commissions. The CPA rates ($100-300 per signup) mean you don't need high volume.

2. **Build the email list NOW.** Newsletter subscribers are worth 10-50x what Twitter followers are worth in direct monetization potential. Every piece of free content should funnel to an email signup.

3. **The subscription model works at small scale.** 200-500 paying subscribers at $8-10/month is $20-60K/year. That's achievable for a quality niche product.

4. **Data products have almost zero marginal cost.** Once you build a projection model, auction calculator, or dashboard, each additional subscriber costs you nothing. This is the best leverage for a solo creator.

5. **The Action Network acquisition ($240M) proves the ceiling is high** for sports betting media, but the realistic opportunity for a solo creator is building to $100-300K/year in revenue, potentially getting acquired or hired by a larger platform if you want.

6. **Twitter/X sponsorship alone won't pay the bills** for accounts under 50K followers. Use Twitter as a distribution channel, not a revenue channel. The money is in what your Twitter audience buys, signs up for, or subscribes to.

7. **Seasonality will crush you if you don't plan for it.** Baseball is a 6-month sport. Either go multi-sport, create evergreen products, or save aggressively during the season.
